# Red-tailed Hawk

*Buteo jamaicensis*

The Red-tailed Hawk is North America's most common and widespread hawk, often seen soaring over open country or perched on telephone poles.

## Physical Description

- **Length**: 45-65 cm (18-26 inches)
- **Wingspan**: 1.1-1.4 m (3.6-4.6 feet)
- **Weight**: 0.7-1.5 kg (1.5-3.3 lbs)

### Plumage Variations

Red-tailed Hawks show extreme variation in plumage:

| Morph | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Light | White chest with dark belly band |
| Dark | Uniformly dark brown body |
| Rufous | Rich reddish-brown underparts |
| Harlan's | Nearly black with white tail mottling |

The characteristic rufous-red tail is present in adults of most morphs.

## Habitat

Highly adaptable, found in:

- Open country with scattered trees
- Prairie and agricultural land
- Desert and scrubland
- Forest edges
- Urban areas

## Diet

Red-tailed Hawks are opportunistic predators:

### Primary Prey
1. **Mammals (85%)**
   - Voles and mice
   - Rabbits and hares
   - Squirrels
   - Prairie dogs

2. **Birds (10%)**
   - Pheasants
   - Blackbirds
   - Starlings

3. **Reptiles (5%)**
   - Snakes
   - Lizards

## Hunting Behavior

### Techniques

- **Soaring**: Circling high to spot prey below
- **Perch hunting**: Watching from elevated posts
- **Stooping**: Diving at high speed
- **Ground pursuit**: Chasing prey on foot

```
Typical hunting day:
- Dawn: Active hunting
- Midday: Soaring on thermals
- Afternoon: Perch hunting
- Dusk: Final hunting bout
```

## Vocalizations

The iconic raptor scream heard in movies is actually a Red-tailed Hawk call:

> **Hollywood Secret**: Movie sound editors use Red-tailed Hawk calls for almost all raptor species, including Bald Eagles (whose actual call is a weak chirp).

## Reproduction

- **Courtship**: Spectacular aerial displays with talon-locking
- **Nest**: Large stick nest, often reused for years
- **Eggs**: 1-5 (usually 2-3)
- **Incubation**: 28-35 days
- **Fledging**: 42-46 days

## Conservation Status

**Status**: Least Concern

Population stable and possibly increasing. Benefits from habitat modification by humans (open areas, perch sites).
